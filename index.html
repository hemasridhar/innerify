Know Yourself

The frameworks behind your inner world

These aren't abstract theories — they are maps of how you think, feel, and react. Explore them with curiosity, not judgment.

Abandonment

The fear of being left behind

A deep conviction that the people you care about will eventually leave, let you down, or choose someone else. It creates a quiet urgency in relationships — a need to hold on tightly or to leave first.

Feels like: Checking your phone constantly when a friend hasn't responded. Assuming silence means rejection.

Defectiveness

The belief that something is fundamentally wrong with you

Not about specific flaws — it's a pervasive sense that if people really knew you, they wouldn't stay. It drives perfectionism, people-pleasing, or withdrawal as shields against being truly seen.

Feels like: Deflecting compliments instantly. Believing your success is luck and your mistakes are proof.

Emotional Deprivation

The quiet ache of unmet emotional needs

A sense that your emotional needs — to be understood, cared for, protected — will never be adequately met by anyone. You may stop asking because you've learned not to expect.

Feels like: Being in a room full of people but feeling fundamentally alone. Not knowing how to ask for comfort.

Subjugation

Sacrificing your needs to keep the peace

Habitually suppressing your own desires, opinions, or feelings because you believe expressing them will lead to conflict, rejection, or punishment. Over time, you may lose touch with what you actually want.

Feels like: Saying "I'm fine with whatever" in every decision. A slow-building resentment you can't quite name.

Unrelenting Standards

Nothing is ever good enough — including you

A relentless internal pressure to meet extremely high standards, often at the cost of your health, relationships, or peace of mind. Achievement feels temporary; the bar always moves upward.

Feels like: Finishing a project and immediately noticing every flaw. Working 12-hour days and still feeling behind.

Mistrust / Abuse

Expecting the worst from others

An expectation that others will hurt, manipulate, or take advantage of you. This isn't paranoia — it's a protective filter developed from real experiences where trust was violated.

Feels like: Analysing every compliment for hidden motives. Keeping people at arm's length even when you want closeness.

Vulnerable Child

The raw, unguarded part of you

This is the emotional core that feels scared, sad, lonely, or overwhelmed. It surfaces when your deepest needs aren't being met — often unexpectedly, in seemingly small moments.

Feels like: Tearing up at a slightly harsh email. Feeling inexplicably small during a disagreement.

Detached Protector

Emotional numbness as a survival strategy

When feelings become too much, this mode shuts them off entirely. You become efficient, logical, and disconnected. It's protective in the moment but isolating over time.

Feels like: Going on autopilot during conflict. Telling yourself you "don't care" when you clearly do.

Punitive Critic

The harsh inner voice that never relents

An internal judge that punishes you for mistakes, imperfections, and vulnerabilities. It often sounds like a demanding parent or authority figure from your past.

Feels like: "You should've known better." "Everyone else manages this easily." "What's wrong with you?"

Compliant Surrenderer

Keeping everyone happy — except yourself

This mode over-adapts to others' expectations, suppressing your own needs to avoid conflict. It masquerades as being easy-going, but beneath it lies unexpressed frustration.

Feels like: Volunteering for extra work you don't want. Agreeing with opinions you privately disagree with.

Healthy Adult

Your most integrated, grounded self

The mode that can observe, reflect, and respond rather than react. It sets boundaries, nurtures the vulnerable child, and quiets the inner critic. Strengthening this mode is the goal.

Feels like: Pausing before reacting. Saying "I need a moment" without guilt. Making choices aligned with your values.

Overcompensator

Fighting the wound by becoming its opposite

Instead of feeling powerless, you dominate. Instead of feeling inadequate, you overachieve aggressively. It's a survival flip — effective short-term, exhausting long-term.

Feels like: Needing to win every argument. Working obsessively to prove doubters wrong.

Type One

The Reformer

"There is a right way, and I must find it."

Principled, purposeful, and self-controlled. Ones have an innate sense of what's right and wrong and an internal drive toward improvement. Their strength is integrity; their challenge is rigidity and self-criticism.

Type Three

The Achiever

"I am what I accomplish."

Adaptive, ambitious, and image-conscious. Threes naturally orient toward goals and success. Their strength is inspiring drive; their challenge is losing touch with their authentic self behind the performance.

Type Five

The Investigator

"If I understand enough, I'll feel safe."

Perceptive, innovative, and private. Fives process the world through knowledge and observation. Their strength is deep insight; their challenge is withdrawing from the very life they're trying to understand.

Type Eight

The Challenger

"I will not be controlled or vulnerable."

Decisive, powerful, and protective. Eights move through the world with directness and intensity. Their strength is courageous leadership; their challenge is vulnerability — the thing they guard against most fiercely.

Patterns & Awareness

Recognise what's running in the background

These are not labels or diagnoses. They are patterns that many high-functioning individuals quietly live with — and rarely name.

Important: This section is for self-awareness and education only. It is not a diagnostic tool and does not replace professional clinical assessment. If something resonates deeply, consider speaking with a qualified mental health professional.

Imposter Phenomenon

Common in high achievers and first-generation professionals

A persistent internal experience of intellectual fraudulence despite clear evidence of competence. You attribute success to luck or timing and live in fear of being "found out."

"I got this role because they couldn't find someone better" — Sound familiar? You're not alone, and you're not a fraud.

Chronic Burnout Pattern

Often mistaken for laziness or loss of motivation

Not just tiredness — a systematic depletion of emotional, cognitive, and physical reserves. It often follows sustained periods where you gave more than you had, without adequate recovery.

"I used to love this work. Now I feel nothing." — Burnout isn't about effort; it's about the absence of recovery.

Anxious Overachievement

Productivity driven by fear rather than passion

Using constant work as an anxiety management strategy. The achievement is real — but the driver is dread, not desire. Rest feels dangerous because silence brings the anxiety to the surface.

"I can't stop working — not because I love it, but because I can't bear what I feel when I stop."

Emotional Avoidance

Efficiency as an emotional escape mechanism

An unconscious pattern of keeping life so structured and busy that there's no room for uncomfortable emotions to surface. When they do, the default response is to solve, minimise, or redirect.

"I don't have time to feel — I have things to do." — But the feelings don't disappear; they just get louder in quiet moments.

Parentification Residue

Carrying responsibility that was never yours to carry

Growing up as the "responsible one," the mediator, or the emotional caretaker of a parent. In adulthood, it shows up as difficulty receiving help, chronic self-reliance, and guilt about having needs.

"I've always taken care of everyone else — I wouldn't even know how to let someone take care of me."

Conflict Avoidance Loop

Peace-keeping at the cost of authenticity

Systematically avoiding confrontation — not because you're calm, but because you fear the consequences of disagreement. Over time, suppressed truths create distance in the very relationships you're trying to protect.

"I'd rather keep the peace than speak my mind" — but the peace you keep isn't peace; it's silence.
