Common questions

You might be wondering

We designed Innerly with care. Here's what people typically ask before they begin.

Is this a diagnosis?
▼
No. Innerly is a self-awareness tool, not a diagnostic service. The assessments we use (like the PHQ-9 or GAD-7) are validated screening instruments — the same ones used in clinical settings — but a screening score is not a diagnosis. Only a licensed mental health professional can provide a clinical diagnosis. Think of Innerly as a mirror that helps you see patterns more clearly, so you can have more informed conversations with a therapist if you choose to.
Is my data private and secure?
▼
Absolutely. Your journal entries and assessment results are stored in your own authenticated Firebase account, encrypted at rest and in transit. We have no ability to read, sell, or share your psychological data. There is no third-party analytics running on your content. If you connect with a therapist through Innerly, you control exactly what is shared — and you can revoke access at any time.
Who is Innerly designed for?
▼
Innerly is for anyone who wants to understand themselves more deeply — entrepreneurs navigating the stress of building, professionals managing complex emotional lives, individuals in therapy who want to track progress between sessions, or simply curious minds drawn to psychology. It's especially valued by those who find traditional journaling too unstructured and want a more guided, evidence-based approach.
Is this a replacement for therapy?
▼
No, and we are intentional about this. Innerly is a self-understanding companion — it helps you explore your inner world between therapy sessions or as a starting point before seeking therapy. We believe self-awareness tools and professional therapy are complementary. If your assessment scores suggest significant distress, we always recommend connecting with a licensed therapist.
Are these real psychological assessments?
▼
Yes. Every assessment in Innerly is a published, peer-reviewed instrument used in clinical practice and research worldwide. The PHQ-9 for depression, GAD-7 for anxiety, PCL-5 for PTSD, the Young Schema Questionnaire, Big Five personality inventory — these are not quizzes we invented. They are scored according to their original validated scoring algorithms, and interpretations follow published clinical guidelines.
I'm going through a difficult time. Will this help?
▼
Innerly can help you understand what you're experiencing and put language to feelings that might otherwise feel overwhelming. Structured journaling and validated screeners can bring clarity. However, if you're in crisis or experiencing thoughts of self-harm, please reach out to a mental health professional or crisis helpline immediately. Innerly is a tool for growth and awareness — not emergency intervention.

Schema Modes
Have you ever snapped at someone and later thought "that wasn't like me"? Schema modes are the emotional states — or inner characters — that get activated when old wounds are triggered. Understanding them is the first step toward responding with intention rather than reacting on autopilot.

🔬 Grounded in: Schema Therapy (Jeffrey Young, 1990). Schema modes are now a core part of the Schema Therapy model and are widely used in treating personality difficulties, chronic depression, and relationship patterns.

Early Maladaptive Schemas
Why do certain situations keep triggering the same emotional reaction? Schemas are deep-seated beliefs about yourself and the world, formed in childhood — often invisibly shaping how you interpret events, choose partners, and react under pressure. Seeing them clearly is where lasting change begins.

🔬 Grounded in: Jeffrey Young's Schema Therapy model identifies 18 early maladaptive schemas across 5 domains. This is one of the most researched frameworks for understanding chronic emotional patterns and personality difficulties.

Attachment Styles
Why do you feel anxious when a partner goes quiet? Why does closeness sometimes feel suffocating? Attachment theory explains how your earliest relationships with caregivers created an invisible blueprint for how you connect with others in adulthood — especially in romantic relationships.
💡 Where does attachment come from?
Attachment patterns form in infancy based on how reliably caregivers responded to your needs. These early experiences create a working model of relationships that you carry — often unconsciously — into adulthood. The good news: attachment is not destiny. "Earned security" is well-documented in research — through therapy, safe relationships, and conscious effort, insecure attachment can evolve into secure connection.
CBT Core Concepts
Ever noticed how one negative thought spirals into a terrible mood, which then leads you to cancel plans, which makes you feel even worse? That's the CBT loop in action. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy gives you tools to interrupt these cycles — at any point.
🔄 The CBT Triangle — and why it matters
Thoughts → Feelings → Behaviours → Thoughts. This loop runs constantly, often outside your awareness. For example: "They didn't text back" (thought) → anxiety (feeling) → checking phone 20 times (behaviour) → "Something is wrong" (thought). CBT teaches you to catch these loops and change the pattern at whichever point is most accessible. That's powerful.
Healthy Relationships
The quality of your relationships is one of the strongest predictors of wellbeing — yet most of us were never taught what "healthy" actually looks like. This section explores the patterns that build connection, the dynamics that erode it, and the communication skills that make the difference.
Personality & Traits
Your personality isn't a label — it's a landscape of tendencies that shape how you think, connect, and navigate the world. Understanding your trait profile helps you recognise your natural strengths, your blind spots in relationships, and which growth approaches are most likely to work for you.
🧬 An important distinction: Traits are not destiny
Personality traits are relatively stable patterns — but they are not fixed. Research shows that sustained therapy, meaningful life experiences, and intentional practice can shift even deeply ingrained tendencies like high neuroticism. Understanding your traits is a starting point for self-awareness, not a life sentence. Think of this as knowing your default settings — so you can choose when to override them.
Understanding Mental Health Conditions 10 categories
Mental health conditions are not character flaws or signs of weakness. They are patterns of thought, feeling, and behaviour that cause significant distress — and they are far more common than most people realise. Learning about them reduces stigma, builds empathy, and can be the first step toward seeking support. Click any card to learn more.

⚠️ Important: This section is for education and awareness only. Reading about a condition is not the same as having it. If you recognise yourself in any description, that's worth exploring with a qualified professional — not a reason to self-diagnose.

Behavioural Psychology 10 concepts
Why do you keep checking your phone even though nothing new has appeared? Why does avoiding something uncomfortable make the urge stronger? Behavioural psychology reveals the invisible mechanisms that shape how habits form, persist, and — most importantly — how they can be deliberately changed.
🧪 Why this matters for you
We are all products of our learning history — but we are not prisoners of it. Understanding concepts like reinforcement, extinction, and shaping gives you the vocabulary and tools to reshape even deeply ingrained responses. These principles underpin CBT, DBT, ACT, and nearly every evidence-based treatment in psychology today.
Therapies & Techniques 12 modalities
Not all therapy is the same — and finding the right approach matters. This section explains the major evidence-based modalities: how they work, who they work best for, and what a session actually feels like. Knowledge of these approaches can help you choose more wisely if you're seeking professional support.
🧠
Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)
Change the thoughts that drive difficult feelings and behaviours
Gold StandardDepressionAnxietyOCDPTSD
▼
🏅 Strongest evidence base of any psychotherapy

CBT is built on the idea that thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are interconnected. By identifying and challenging distorted automatic thoughts (cognitive restructuring) and gradually confronting feared situations (behavioural experiments), CBT breaks vicious cycles and builds more adaptive coping.

Core Techniques
  • Thought records & cognitive restructuring
  • Behavioural activation
  • Exposure & Response Prevention (ERP)
  • Problem-solving training
  • Socratic questioning
What to Expect

12–20 structured weekly sessions. Collaborative — you and the therapist work as a team. Skills-based: tools are yours to keep for life. Homework between sessions is a core component.

Best For
  • Depression & low mood
  • GAD, panic, phobias
  • OCD (with ERP)
  • Eating disorders (CBT-E)
  • Health anxiety
Limitations

Can feel formulaic; less effective when trauma is the root cause. Requires active engagement and willingness to do between-session work.

🌊
Dialectical Behaviour Therapy (DBT)
Radical acceptance + practical change skills for emotional storms
BPDSelf-harmEmotion regulationEating disorders
▼
🏅 First-line for BPD; reduces self-harm & suicidality

Developed by Marsha Linehan, DBT balances acceptance (you are doing the best you can) with change (you can do better). The biosocial theory holds that BPD arises from biological emotional sensitivity combined with an invalidating environment. DBT teaches four skill modules to create a "life worth living."

4 Skill Modules
  • 🧘 Mindfulness — core foundation
  • 🌡️ Distress Tolerance — crisis survival
  • ❤️ Emotion Regulation — understanding feelings
  • 🤝 Interpersonal Effectiveness — relationships
Format

Individual weekly therapy + weekly skills group + phone coaching. Full programme lasts 6–12 months. Requires high commitment from both client and therapist.

Key Techniques
  • TIPP (Temperature, Intense exercise, Paced breathing, Progressive relaxation)
  • DEAR MAN (assertiveness)
  • Opposite Action
  • Radical Acceptance
Best For
  • Borderline Personality Disorder
  • Chronic self-harm / suicidality
  • Eating disorders
  • Substance abuse with emotional dysregulation
👁️
EMDR
Eye Movement Desensitisation & Reprocessing for trauma
PTSDTraumaAnxietyComplex trauma
▼
🏅 WHO-recommended first-line treatment for PTSD

EMDR uses bilateral stimulation (typically eye movements) while the client holds a traumatic memory in mind. This activates the brain's natural processing system — similar to REM sleep — allowing the memory to be "digested" and stored in a less emotionally charged form. No detailed verbal retelling of the trauma is required.

8 Phases
  • History taking & treatment planning
  • Preparation & resourcing
  • Assessment of target memory
  • Desensitisation (bilateral stimulation)
  • Installation of positive belief
  • Body scan, Closure, Re-evaluation
Best For
  • Single-incident trauma (accidents, assaults)
  • Complex developmental trauma
  • Phobias & performance anxiety
  • Grief and loss
What to Expect

6–12 sessions for single-incident trauma; longer for complex trauma. Sessions can be intense. Therapist must be specifically EMDR-trained and accredited.

How It Works

Bilateral stimulation appears to activate working memory competing with the trauma memory's vividness, allowing the adaptive information processing (AIP) system to integrate fragmented material.

🗂️
Schema Therapy
Heal the deep core beliefs formed in childhood
Personality disordersChronic depressionBPDRelationship patterns
▼
✅ Strong evidence for BPD and personality disorders

Schema Therapy integrates CBT with attachment theory, psychodynamic insights, and Gestalt experiential techniques. It targets 18 Early Maladaptive Schemas — deeply-rooted beliefs like "I am unlovable" — that form when core childhood needs go unmet. The therapist uses limited reparenting to provide a corrective emotional experience.

Core Concepts
  • 18 Early Maladaptive Schemas (EMS)
  • Schema Modes (Child, Parent, Adult modes)
  • 5 core emotional needs
  • 3 coping styles: surrender, avoidance, overcompensation
Key Techniques
  • Limited reparenting
  • Imagery rescripting
  • Chair work (mode dialogue)
  • Empathic confrontation
  • Cognitive schema restructuring
Best For
  • BPD, Narcissistic, Avoidant PD
  • Chronic depression & anxiety
  • Recurring relationship problems
  • Clients who haven't responded to standard CBT
Duration

Longer-term: typically 1–4 years of weekly sessions. Deep work — expect to explore childhood memories, feel emotions fully, and gradually build the Healthy Adult mode.

🌿
Acceptance & Commitment Therapy (ACT)
Stop fighting your mind — build a rich, meaningful life instead
AnxietyOCDChronic painDepressionBurnout
▼
✅ Third-wave CBT with strong meta-analytic support

ACT belongs to the "third wave" of CBT. Rather than changing thoughts, ACT teaches psychological flexibility — the ability to contact the present moment fully, open up to difficult experiences, and pursue what truly matters. The goal is not to feel better but to live better, even alongside pain.

6 Core Processes (Hexaflex)
  • Acceptance (make room for feelings)
  • Defusion (unhook from thoughts)
  • Present-moment contact
  • Self-as-context (observer self)
  • Values clarification
  • Committed action
Key Techniques
  • Leaves on a stream (defusion)
  • The struggle switch metaphor
  • Values bullseye exercise
  • Willing hands / expansion
Best For
  • OCD (alongside ERP)
  • Chronic pain & illness
  • Anxiety disorders
  • Work burnout and stress
Duration

Flexible: 8–16 sessions, or longer. Also delivered in group format. Self-help books like "The Happiness Trap" (Russ Harris) make it very accessible.

🫂
Internal Family Systems (IFS)
Understand and heal the 'parts' inside you
TraumaSelf-esteemInner criticPersonality disorders
▼
📘 Emerging evidence base; NREPP recognised

IFS posits that the mind is naturally multiple — composed of "parts" (sub-personalities) that each developed to protect the core Self from pain. Exile parts carry wounded feelings; Protectors work to keep exiles out of awareness. Healing happens when the compassionate Self leads and parts feel safe to unburden.

Core Concepts
  • Self: 8 Cs (Calm, Curiosity, Clarity, Compassion, Confidence, Creativity, Courage, Connectedness)
  • Exiles: hurt, young parts
  • Managers: pre-emptive protectors
  • Firefighters: reactive parts (addiction, self-harm)
Best For
  • Trauma & complex PTSD
  • Harsh inner critic / self-hate
  • Eating disorders
  • Shame and low self-worth
  • Addiction recovery
Process
  • Find a part (identify internally)
  • Focus on it with curiosity
  • Flesh it out (image, age, feeling)
  • Befriend and ask what it needs
  • Unburden extreme beliefs
What to Expect

Introspective and experiential. Sessions involve turning attention inward and "talking to" parts. Works well alongside somatic approaches. Therapist must be IFS-trained.

🫀
Somatic Experiencing (SE)
Trauma is stored in the body — healing happens there too
PTSDTraumaChronic stressDissociation
▼
📘 Growing evidence base; complements EMDR & IFS well

Developed by Peter Levine, SE is based on the observation that animals "shake off" trauma by completing interrupted defensive responses. Humans suppress these responses, leaving residual activation trapped in the body. SE guides clients to track body sensations, titrate activation, and allow the nervous system to complete its natural healing cycle.

Key Concepts
  • SIBAM (Sensation, Image, Behaviour, Affect, Meaning)
  • Titration (small doses of trauma material)
  • Pendulation (safety ↔ activation)
  • Resourcing (building safe anchors)
Related Modalities
  • Sensorimotor Psychotherapy
  • EMDR (with somatic focus)
  • Hakomi (mindful body-centred)
  • Trauma-Sensitive Yoga
  • Polyvagal-informed therapy
Best For
  • Developmental trauma
  • Complex PTSD
  • Dissociation & freeze states
  • Clients who find talk therapy insufficient
What to Expect

Slower, very gentle. Therapist tracks breath, posture, gestures, and micro-movements. Sensations (tingling, warmth, trembling) are welcomed as signs of healing.

🧘
Mindfulness-Based Therapies (MBSR / MBCT)
Observe your mind without judgement — break the relapse cycle
Depression relapseAnxietyStressChronic pain
▼
🏅 MBCT reduces depression relapse by ~40–50%

MBSR is an 8-week programme using formal meditation, body scan, and mindful movement to reduce stress. MBCT adapts MBSR for depression, teaching clients to recognise and "decenter" from depressive thought patterns before they spiral into relapse.

Core Practices
  • Body scan meditation (45 min daily)
  • Sitting meditation (breath/sounds/thoughts)
  • Mindful movement (yoga/walking)
  • 3-minute breathing space
Key Principles
  • Decentering: "thoughts are not facts"
  • Non-judgmental awareness
  • Beginner's mind
  • Present-moment orientation
Best For
  • Preventing depression relapse (3+ episodes)
  • Generalised anxiety
  • Chronic pain & fatigue
  • Stress at work / burnout
Format

Standardised 8-week group programme, 2.5 hrs per week + all-day retreat. Requires ~45 min daily home practice. Also available digitally via apps.

🌙
Psychodynamic Therapy
Explore the unconscious roots of current struggles
DepressionAnxietyRelationship patternsIdentity
▼
✅ Comparable to CBT for depression; superior for complex presentations

Rooted in psychoanalytic theory, psychodynamic therapy explores how unconscious conflicts, early relational patterns, and unresolved grief shape present-day suffering. The therapeutic relationship becomes a live laboratory — patterns in the transference reveal core relational expectations that can be examined and changed.

Core Techniques
  • Free association
  • Transference interpretation
  • Dream exploration
  • Resistance analysis
  • Linking past to present
Key Concepts
  • Unconscious conflict
  • Defence mechanisms
  • Object relations
  • Attachment patterns
  • Therapeutic alliance
Best For
  • Longstanding depression or anxiety
  • Repeated relationship problems
  • Grief, loss, life transitions
  • People wanting deeper self-understanding
Duration

Short-term (16–20 sessions; STDP) or long-term (1–3+ years). Open-ended and less structured than CBT. More exploratory than directive.

🪞
Mentalisation-Based Treatment (MBT)
Learn to understand your own and others' minds more clearly
BPDAttachmentRelationshipsSelf-harm
▼
✅ Robust evidence for BPD; 8-year follow-up shows lasting gains

Developed by Bateman and Fonagy, MBT targets mentalisation — the capacity to understand behaviour in terms of mental states (thoughts, feelings, desires, intentions). In BPD, mentalisation collapses under stress, leading to impulsive action. MBT helps rebuild this capacity through a supportive therapeutic relationship.

Core Skills Developed
  • Identifying and naming mental states
  • Perspective-taking under stress
  • Noticing mentalisation failure
  • Slowing down emotional reactivity
  • Exploring interpersonal misunderstandings
Best For
  • Borderline Personality Disorder
  • Attachment difficulties
  • Chronic self-harm
  • Eating disorders
  • Antisocial PD
Format

Combines individual (weekly) and group therapy (3× per week). 18-month programme in standard form. Also adapted for adolescents (MBT-A) and families (MBT-F).

Therapist Stance

Not-knowing, inquisitive, curious — the therapist models good mentalisation by wondering aloud about mental states rather than interpreting or advising.

🗣️
Motivational Interviewing (MI)
Resolve ambivalence and find your own reasons to change
Substance useBehaviour changeEating disordersHealth behaviour
▼
🏅 Most studied brief intervention for addiction

MI is a collaborative, person-centred counselling style that strengthens a person's own motivation to change by exploring and resolving ambivalence. Rather than pushing change, the therapist elicits "change talk" — the person's own arguments for change — and uses reflective listening to amplify it.

OARS Skills
  • Open questions
  • Affirmations
  • Reflective listening
  • Summarisations
4 Processes
  • Engaging (building alliance)
  • Focusing (setting agenda)
  • Evoking (eliciting change talk)
  • Planning (commitment & action)
Best For
  • Substance use disorders
  • Pre-contemplation / resistance to change
  • Eating disorder engagement
  • Medication adherence
Format

Can be delivered in 1–4 sessions or as part of longer treatment. Often used at entry to treatment to build readiness, or as a precursor to CBT or DBT.

💚
Compassion Focused Therapy (CFT)
Soothe the threat system with self-compassion and warmth
ShameSelf-criticismDepressionTrauma
▼
✅ Strong for shame-based presentations; growing evidence base

Developed by Paul Gilbert, CFT is grounded in evolutionary understanding of three emotion-regulation systems: threat, drive, and soothing. Many people with mental health difficulties have a hyperactive threat system and underdeveloped soothing system. CFT uses compassionate mind training to activate the affiliative/soothing system.

3 Emotion Systems
  • 🔴 Threat (protect, avoid)
  • 🔵 Drive (seek, achieve)
  • 🟢 Soothing/affiliative (care, connect)
Compassionate Mind Training
  • Compassionate attention
  • Compassionate reasoning
  • Compassionate imagery (Compassionate Self)
  • Compassionate letter writing
Best For
  • High shame & self-criticism
  • Eating disorders
  • Depression with self-loathing
  • Trauma survivors
  • Perfectionists & high achievers
What to Expect

Psychoeducation about brain threat systems helps normalise suffering. Imagery exercises can initially feel strange. For some, self-compassion initially triggers "compassion fatigue" — a recognised response therapists are trained to work with.

🧪 Self-Understanding Assessments

Validated psychological instruments — scored and interpreted to help you see yourself more clearly.

These are not quizzes — they are the same validated screening tools used in clinical practice and research worldwide. Each assessment measures a specific dimension of your psychological experience, and your results are scored using published clinical algorithms.

Your results are for self-awareness and reflection. They are not a clinical diagnosis. If any result concerns you, we encourage you to share it with a licensed mental health professional.
